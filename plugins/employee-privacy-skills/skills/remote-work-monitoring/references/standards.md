# Standards and Regulatory References — Remote Work Monitoring

## Primary Legislation

### ECHR Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private Life and the Home
- Art. 8(1): Right to respect for private life, family life, home, and correspondence.
- The home environment receives the highest level of protection under Art. 8.
- Monitoring technologies that capture the home environment constitute interference with Art. 8(1).

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights Article 7
- Right to respect for private and family life, home, and communications.

### GDPR
- Art. 5(1)(c): Data minimisation — monitoring data must be limited to what is necessary.
- Art. 6(1)(f): Legitimate interest — primary basis; requires rigorous balancing test in home context.
- Art. 13/14: Transparency — employees must be informed before monitoring.
- Art. 22: Automated decision-making — applies to AI-powered productivity scoring.
- Art. 35: DPIA required — systematic monitoring, vulnerable subjects, innovative technology.

## National DPA Positions

### CNIL (France)
- Questions & Answers on teleworking (2020, updated 2022).
- Continuous monitoring disproportionate for remote workers.
- Screen capture, keystroke logging, webcam surveillance prohibited for general monitoring.
- Connection logs and output-based metrics acceptable.

### ICO (United Kingdom)
- Employment Practices Code, Part 3: monitoring must be proportionate.
- Technologies deployed to check desk presence unlikely proportionate.
- Output-based management recommended.

### Garante (Italy)
- Workers' Statute Art. 4 applies to remote monitoring.
- Trade union agreement required for remote monitoring equipment.
- Continuous screen monitoring prohibited.

### BfDI (Germany)
- BDSG Section 26 applies to remote monitoring.
- Works council co-determination Section 87(1)(6) BetrVG.
- Keylogger evidence inadmissible if monitoring disproportionate.

### AEPD (Spain)
- Art. 87-90 LOPDGDD digital rights in employment.
- GPS tracking of remote workers outside working hours prohibited.

## Case Law

### Chetu Inc v Employee (Tilburg District Court, Netherlands, 2022)
- Employee dismissed for refusing to keep webcam on during remote work.
- Court ruled: continuous webcam monitoring is disproportionate invasion of privacy.
- Dismissal was wrongful; EUR 75,000 compensation awarded.

### Barbulescu v Romania (ECHR Grand Chamber, 2017)
- Six-factor proportionality test applies to all employee monitoring including remote.
- Heightened application in home context.
